NON-PROFIT CAFE EXPLODES IN POPULARITY AFTER VIRAL REVIEW

by Fox Huang

Over the last two weeks, popular social media influencer Sorca Callahan uploaded a series of short video clips to Instagram listing her favourite ‘Underground Coffee Shops’. Fans and followers of Callahan loved hearing her opinions and recommendations on the best little-known coffee shops all across Sydney’s CDB.

Sorca’s fans greatly appreciate her honest evaluation of the cuisine and service and can’t wait to visit the places she recommended.

Many of the cafes featured in the videos have taken to social media to thank Callahan for the review and to welcome all their new customers. Cafe la dinde sans plumes tweeted, “A warm hug to all the new fans who’ve just discovered us! thanks @sorcacall!”

But the business that seems to have gone the most viral after Callahan’s reviews was the tiny Rose Cafe; a store with a surprisingly long history and a powerful connection to a rather odd flower.

“I guess it all started with FORC,” said Glenn Moss, manager of the Rose Cafe. FORC (Formal Organisation for Rose Conservation), Moss explained to me, is a non-profit organisation founded over 40 years ago for the sole purpose of the conservation of… rather surprisingly… the endangered Rose flower. The plant is a rather ugly, squat bush of tangled thorns with tight, soft flower petals. Though it used to be an invasive weed, the Rose has long since lost ability to grow in our modern soils. “The Rose just doesn’t take to our earth today, there’s the wrong amount of plastics in our soil or something.”

Curious to learn more about this strange, rare plant; I reached out to Danika Goddard, Rose expert and treasurer of FORC. She told me a bit about the Rose’s historical and symbolic significance. In the past, a Rose has meant anything from a romantic gesture of love, to the name of a civil war. “The Rose has played a powerful role in human history,” said Goddard. Which brings us to the Rose’s new found fame in the modern day. “Rose Cafe started out of necessity about eight years ago, we were running low on investors and chances for the Rose’s survival were looking grim. Coffee shops were going through a bit of a boom at the time so it seemed the obvious choice.” 100% of the profits from the store go towards helping to improve soil conditions for Roses and raise awareness about the plant’s endangered status. “We also sell fantastic Rose related merchandise from the cafe like Rose shirts and Rose tote-bags,” said Moss, “It’s great to know when you buy our merch products, or coffee, or RoseTea™ you’re helping to keep this beautiful flower from extinction.”

I asked Moss what he thought of the store’s recent surge in popularity, “Yes, me and our staff have all been very excited, though a bit overwhelmed by all the new customers.” When I asked if he had seen Sorca Callahan’s review he laughed, “Is that who made the video? Our staff have been sharing it around but I’m yet to see it.” Goddard said she hadn’t seen the video either but said she was glad more people were falling in love with the Rose, “It’s the flower of love, after all.”

The hashtag #RoseCafe has been trending over the weekend, with new fans everywhere taking to social media to share the love.

However, not all fans are happy about Sorca Callahan’s viral series of posts. Many feel she should have left the stores underground rather than wrenching them into the mainstream.

I reached out to Sorca for her thoughts on both the controversy and the plant but she was unavailable for comment.

Fox is a freelance journalist studying Marketing at UNSW.
